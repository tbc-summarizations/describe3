They boasted a defense anchored by Nemanja Vidić, at the time a rock at Manchester United. Their midfield had Miloš Krasić, a rising star in Russia. Their Dutch manager, Arno Pijpers, probably felt like he was leading a bunch of sheep to the slaughter. Yet, somehow, against all logic, the Kazakhs pulled off a 2-1 victory. Kairat Ashirbekov and Nurbol Zhumaskaliev scored, sending the home crowd into a frenzy and leaving Serbian fans in utter disbelief. Žigić's goal was a mere consolation, a face-saving act in a night of humiliation. This match screams complacency from Serbia. Did they underestimate Kazakhstan? Absolutely. Did they play with the intensity and focus required for a crucial qualifier? Clearly not. Vidić might have been a world-class defender, but even he couldn't prevent the embarrassment that unfolded in Almaty. The midfield failed to control the game, and the attack lacked the cutting edge needed to break down a determined Kazakh defense. As for Kazakhstan, you have to give them credit for their resilience and determination. They may not have had the individual talent of their opponents, but they played with heart and took their chances when they came. David Loria, the goalkeeper, probably had the game of his life, and the defense managed to keep the Serbian attack at bay for long stretches. Ultimately, this match serves as a harsh reminder that anything can happen in football. It's a story of a massive underdog upsetting a complacent favorite, a night where Kazakhstan defied expectations and Serbia crumbled under pressure. For Kazakhstan, it was a moment of glory; for Serbia, a dark stain on their footballing history.
